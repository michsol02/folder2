Building Consensus

In 2012 and 2013, in our quest to help businesses to communicate accurate information about actual, sensible, valuable policies and practices concerning important social and environmental issues, we sat experts and campaigners from NGOs and think tanks down with business decision makers.

We facilitated frank and honest discussions, often on difficult and contested issues. What quickly became clear is that both ‘sides’ shared real passion for issues of common interest and focus. And, often surprisingly, they recognised that they had so much more in common with those on “the other side” than they’d ever have thought possible.  

Responsible 100 scorecards aim to give a comprehensive account of what POOR, OKAY, GOOD and EXCELLENT performance looks like on any given social, environmental or governance issue, factoring in a multitude of sources, pools of knowledge and experience, views and beliefs. 

They seek to categorise all the policies and practices described by businesses and organisations in the information they submit, as well as those observed in the wider world. 

As this old illustration sought to show, the work of constructing and developing scorecards has always been a team effort, collaborative and consensual. And long will it remain so.

People’s Assembly

Civil society organisations and citizens have critical roles to play in improving the responsibility practices and operational effectiveness of businesses.  

From mental health and wellbeing in the workplace, to how businesses operate customer and complaints procedures, to charitable giving, to human rights abuse in supply chains, to avoiding tax, to GHG emissions - everyone has a view as to what matters.  

And everyone has a view as to what unacceptable practices are, and conversely what are the best possible approaches that companies take, or could take with adequate imagination, daring and - to bring us back to Earth - resources.   

The Responsible 100 People’s Assembly is a community of those willing to assume an active role in identifying and/or debating those responsibility issues that should be considered as high priority, and to what constitutes POOR, OKAY, GOOD and EXCELLENT standards of practice on those issues.  

Responsible 100’s unique approach originated in a desire to build consensus between businesses and their stakeholders on the various social, environmental and ethical issues that affect us all.  

We embrace the inclusivity and diversity of experience and expertise across our community. Since the beginning, we’ve brokered relationships between passionate practitioners and experts from and between SMEs, large corporations, NGO’s, governmental bodies, academia, and beyond.  

On a regular basis, we survey individual members of the People’s Assembly and gather public opinion data from a range of sources. And we’ve built and continue to develop tools to allow consumers and employees - a.k.a. citizens - to comment, rate, suggest and propose, to make Responsible 100 as open and democratic as possible.  

The Responsible 100 People’s Assembly acts as a clearing house where public priorities and expectations are set against what actually happens inside businesses, with a view to constantly narrowing the gap between the two.
